# How I sailed the Atlantic
#### date: 27.06.2026 12:00

> "— A timeline of the clusterfuck I got myself into." — that's how the audio message I recorded on April 15th began, lying in my cabin. I had 20 minutes left until my next watch. I'd more or less come back to my senses, but I was still eyeing the food with suspicion.

But let's rewind about a month.

On March 11th I got an email with a pretty interesting offer from the captain I'd trained under in Tallinn: to sail a yacht along the route **Tenerife — Madeira — Lagos** or **Lagos — Cádiz — Gibraltar — Cartagena — Ibiza**. You could pick either one leg, or do the whole route, which would take about 20 days.

The price was very reasonable — I'd even say cheap: 850 euros, including the yacht charter, fuel, moorings, and food for any one leg of the trip. One yacht, led by an experienced captain.

I'd actually thought about a trip like this before: it would be great to gain experience alongside seasoned sailors with tens of thousands of nautical miles behind them. I was also curious how my body would react to a long passage.

A year ago we'd sailed around the islands of Vis and Korčula in Croatia — that was fun, but we spent every night in a marina, walked around, drank wine, and overall it was a nice break from the usual hustle, and we picked up a little bit of sailing experience.

So, I had to decide quickly, since the start was exactly a month away. I thought it over for about 10 days, worked out the vacation days, the tickets to Tenerife, and finally gave a positive answer.

I book the tickets:

- Departure April 10th, Riga — Warsaw — Tenerife South
- Return April 21st, Lisbon — Copenhagen — Riga

## Next, Packing

What clothing to buy, devices, a first-aid kit, what to take from home, and so on. Decathlon became my best friend for a while, although the selection for sailors in Riga turned out to be very poor. Honestly, I didn't really want to spend money on expensive brand-name gear (which I'd regret later, of course). There's no point listing the entire packing list here.

One cool thing — I bought myself a [Garmin InReach Messenger](https://www.garmin.com/en-US/p/793265/), since it wasn't clear whether we'd have Starlink, and even if we did, whether we'd have an ocean plan. There are 9 of us, including the captain.
I met Sergey and Evgeny back at Warsaw airport, and another Zhenya — already at Tenerife airport.

The four of us arrived a day early, and the captain had promised us a place to sleep on one of the free yachts in the marina. That's how it worked out — they gave us bedding and a towel, we moved onto the yacht, and went to wander around town looking for something to eat, fueling up in between the fuss with — what else — San Miguel.

## Saturday, April 11th

Shower, coffee, breakfast, more coffee. We dump our stuff onto our actual yacht, but it hasn't been cleaned or prepped yet — they promise to have it ready by 16:00, which is also when the general meeting of all the passage participants is set.

Catching a glimpse of the mess on our yacht, we start to doubt it can realistically be cleaned by 16:00.

We have breakfast, and now we've got about 4 hours to do our own thing — some sat down to work, Zhenya and Sergey took a taxi to El Médano, and I decided to just walk to Playa de la Tejita on foot, about 5 km.

![](https://i.vas3k.club/9d527e00f199131b10262f1e20c7e9c097ae0f69aee68a2bbf2097fb60b20145.jpg)

Having refueled along the way with dorada and ensalada de atún, I was already hurrying back to the meeting at the marina. I run into captain Igor and Maxim having lunch at a café near the marina.

Mark and Volodya are already waiting for us at the marina. Our boat is delayed.

We discuss the plan. And the plan is this: accept the boat, check that everything's in order and there's enough of everything, buy food, fill the water, fuel up the jerry cans and lash them down, and head out at night toward Madeira. The plan is simple, which is why it's beautiful (c).

We decide to go for groceries; the captain hands out a list of what to buy. The assortment is simple: meat, ground meat, beans, juices, sweets, potatoes, grains, pasta, eggs, vegetables, fruit, water, and so on (60 bottles of 1.5 liters each — importantly with plastic labels, so they don't disintegrate under the floorboards and clog the pumps).

## Sunday, April 12th

Slept badly — a choir of snoring men is a dubious treat for the ears.
The plan is to wait for the technician to look at the head, and meanwhile more little defects start piling up that it'd be good to fix:

- The bimini is wobbly
- The anchor needs to be secured
- The sprayhood is also holding poorly

In short, all of this takes time, and usually everything gets fixed with whatever's on hand: Sikaflex, reinforced tape, WD-40 (video).

The guys from SailMe respond quickly, but we're not the only ones they're dealing with.

![](https://i.vas3k.club/d282f13306c3ffadf14d72f5cdfce90181fcfbcea38dd7ef91d3f059da8375b2.jpg)

We seem ready to head out, but then the question of lunch comes up — a very important question — and to save time Kirill orders pizza. They promise it'll all be there in 25 minutes, but let's not forget we're in Spain.

The captain runs a briefing; we split into watches of three. My watches are 18:00–22:00 and 6:00–10:00 a.m.

16:00, and here we are, all in life jackets, ready to go.

We're heading along the east coast of Tenerife; the forecast gives us a headwind up to 17 m/s and a nasty swell, but the plan is to tack up to the northern tip of Tenerife and then settle onto a pleasant course toward Madeira. We just have to suffer a little.

![](https://i.vas3k.club/43a06ef7c1663e35fe402d3a7fcc769f8a8c9be89339c80877fcd3f8c0d262c2.jpg)

So in we go. We'd just left the marina when I immediately realized the swell was brutally big. I was up on the bow at the time, stowing the fenders — and crawled back to the cockpit on my knees on a safety line, because it was genuinely scary.

Then for some reason we got the command to set the jib first instead of the main — okay, whatever. Jib's up. We start setting the main, and our main is on a furler — that's a real pain, especially with sails that have seen some life. We try to unfurl the main, but it won't go, jamming halfway, and no matter how hard we try, nothing works. We didn't need the full main in that wind anyway, but we still needed more.

However, we know that at the northern tip of Tenerife, which we should pass around 22:00, a breeze awaits us — and not just a breeze, but again a pretty serious one with gusts up to that same 17 m/s.

My watch started at 18:00, it was hot, and I decided it was a bit too early to put on the foul-weather trousers.

But as soon as we started working our way further and further north, the wind began to build, the sun was setting fast, and there — really, in just 5 minutes — the situation changes dramatically: the sun set, a gale, the captain commands "head to wind!" — we set the main!

I take the winch that furls it, and Kirill the one that unfurls it, since we know our main has its quirks and we probably won't set it on the first try.

And so it is. The helmsman holds head-to-wind, we try to work the main out somehow back and forth. Honestly, I'm already plenty tired, and then Kirill somehow finds the position where the main comes out on its own with the wave — you just have to give it an impulse.

While I was working the winch, I caught a couple of solid splashes. Being without the foul-weather trousers, I instantly turned into a wet mess; my jacket turned out to be a joke.

And on top of that it got brutally cold, the wind picked up, and it got dark — all of this in half an hour!

We set the main and the jib, and soaking wet, I make a fatal mistake — I go down to the cabin and try to put on the foul-weather trousers. It's practically impossible; the rolling is insane, the heel is severe. I start to panic a little. Realizing I can't stay in the cabin any longer, I run out to the cockpit, where four people are already throwing up over the side!

I clip in, sit down on the leeward side, and that's when I "went under," and I only remember Mark belaying me as I feed the fish.

After that it seems to ease up, but again — night, can't see a damn thing, impossible to fix your eyes on anything on the horizon.

Then I realize I'm so cold that I've got a cramp in my wrist! My wrist, man! I can't unclench my wrist! I genuinely tensed up, wondering what the hell is happening to me — and I decide to go warm up below in the saloon after all, since I'm soaked through with sweat and water. I lie down in the saloon with my head positioned to feel the boat's maneuvers as much as possible.

[Briskly, with a heel](https://youtube.com/shorts/aOVsd-2Jsl8?feature=share)

Then Kirill runs over and starts shaking me, asking me questions. He actually thought I was having a stroke or a heart attack — asked me to smile and all that.

I have no idea what time it is, probably around 1:30 a.m. I somehow make my way to my forward cabin, strip everything off, and wrap myself in a blanket. I try to warm up, my teeth are chattering against each other — and that's not a figure of speech, it's freaking cold.

Sleeping on the waves in the forward cabin in a sharp swell is very hard — you get tossed up and then slammed back onto a hard mattress.
A kind of kidney massage I never ordered.

I crashed for about 3 hours in that state, but I warmed up!

It was around 4:00 a.m., and my watch started at 6:00.
I was badly nauseous and really wanted to drink.

Lying in the cabin, on that insane rolling, I thought: "what the hell yachting, what did you even get yourself into." I thought that was it — I'd reach Madeira and take the first bus to Lisbon and be done.

But I had to somehow get to this Madeira, and ahead were God knows how many watches, and getting ready for my second watch took me an hour.

First I put on all the thermals, which took about 10 minutes — you try to read the wave period and use the lulls between the amplitudes.
Same approach with everything else; you always have to remember three points of contact, and on top of that you're nauseous.

## Tuesday, April 14th

By 6 a.m. I come out to the cockpit; Kirill asks how I'm doing.
Overall, seems okay. In the fresh air it's definitely better. But the wind is brutal, the waves are nearly 3 meters, the water just floods over the deck.

Coming out to the cockpit, I learned the news that the jib had torn again — a gust hit. It tore quite conveniently, in a way that let us reef it, and it was still useful.

After a while seasickness makes itself known — well, you get the idea.

I somehow survive this watch and retreat to the cabin, where for the next 8 hours I'd fight my desire to get out of here. I didn't use the toilet, didn't brush my teeth, didn't wash my face, didn't eat — I just drank water and lay there.

By the way, using the toilet while you're hurling is its own kind of pleasure, and you put it off until the last possible moment.

This day's 18:00–22:00 watch passed I don't remember how — there's one video and that's it for the last 24 hours, that's all! — [here it is](https://youtube.com/shorts/vSpyIlvkOFs)

The main task on watch was keeping an eye on the jib; you had to be ready to ease the boom if a gust hit.

When the sun set, we periodically lit up the sail with flashlights and checked whether it was "flogging." Overall it was at least something to do, since otherwise the steering came down to holding course by the wind, until we found the wind autopilot on the plotter.

There's very little crossing of other vessels in the ocean.

So, on this day at 22:12 we were somewhere around here.

## Wednesday, April 15th

I wake up again around 5 a.m. and get ready for watch. I feel I've got noticeably more strength; I come up and offer the guys in the cockpit tea or coffee — everyone agrees.

Honestly, this routine — "coffee, tea, sandwich" — is the bedrock of existence. In any unclear situation, offer your neighbor a cup of tea.

I don't remember anything notable until lunch — the rolling, the heel, the sleep deprivation are a bit wearing.

Sometime that day I voice my idea of getting off at Madeira to the captain. He, in a rather philosophical mood, suggested I leave my earthly affairs on land and focus on the passage.

Also, the captain noticed I had a crappy jacket and offered me his sailing one, for which a huge thank you to him. It was a complete game-changer.

Second thing: the captain says,

> If you can't sleep in the forward cabin, go crash in mine — I've got a free spot.

The captain slept in the "closet," with 2 bunks like in a third-class train car.
Oh, how I enjoyed sleeping in a bed that didn't beat me up like a cutlet.

That same evening life started to look up: I had a simple boiled potato and managed to eat, dolphins came to us, and we ran around like kids filming little videos, and that same evening, toward the end of my watch, we saw the lights of Madeira — but it was still far away...

## Friday, April 17th

My first dog watch began. We were all very tired, because none of us had managed to rest properly during the day.

We set the sails, switch off the engine, and revel in the silence; we got wind and were moving briskly at 7–8 knots.

I wanted to sleep desperately. The wind was strong, and it was pretty cold, plus the constant dampness in the cockpit from the spray adds to the chill. It's impossible to sit — you freeze and start fighting off sleep, but we periodically swap with the helmsman; you have to keep an eye on the course and the sails, after all, it's a responsible job entrusted to us. To keep from losing our cognitive abilities somehow, we started playing the cities game. I never would have thought it could be so engaging.

Around 4 a.m., toward the end of the watch, the captain commanded a tack, and that means hustle and sail work, while you actually want to sleep, friends — so in the end I had to spend an extra hour with the guys from the next watch.

### Morning

I wake up and walk straight into some commotion in the cockpit. It turns out the fuel jerry cans, which were stowed in a locker, had toppled onto each other because we were sailing at a strong heel, and fuel started getting squeezed out of them.

We had to take it all out, clean it, improvise extra gaskets out of plastic bags — in short, the bedrock of yachting: something is always breaking, leaking, farting, whistling.

## Saturday, April 18th

I'm coming on for another watch. We took the procedures on the boat pretty seriously: before coming on watch, the previous watch hands us the info — the situation, wind, speed, course. Then at the end of the watch we write in the log how far we went, the wave height, what the wind was, the course, and we also wrote down how long the engine ran, since the fuel sensors weren't working.

Actually, not a single sensor worked — not fuel, not water. At least it showed the battery charge level. We periodically started the engine to recharge the batteries. We had a solar panel, but there was little sun, and again it wasn't clear whether it was charging anything or not.

On this watch we steered by the stars, heading toward the North Star, i.e. course 0°.

That wasn't what we needed, but we still had to head further north. For two days now we were unlucky with the wind, and it blew right at us. The wind was good, and we often joked "going well, but the wrong way."

## Sunday, April 19th

Conceptually, few changes — the same maddening rolling, slamming into the waves, the impossibility of getting proper sleep. The night watch was tough; we stood there, freezing. After the 4 a.m. watch I also had to clean the stove, because the burners were flooded with water and I really wanted some warm tea. After the tea I went to sleep in the captain's cabin.
After my watch came the captain and Zhenya's watch. Zhenya is an experienced racing sailor, and he and the captain squeezed the maximum out of the sails and the yacht. However, by morning the wind died down, they switched on the engine, and oh bliss — I somehow slept and my mood improved.

## Monday, April 20th

We come on for the night watch; the captain is sleeping, but there was an order to wake him if the wind picked up and the sails needed setting.

We decide to show a bit of initiative and set the sails ourselves. Kirill is an experienced sailor, but again — night and all that. Mark from the previous watch helps us. We unfurl the jib, the wind is almost 13 m/s.

[Wind picked up, we wake the captain](https://youtube.com/shorts/dxlYGDLXrNg)

![](https://i.vas3k.club/47571974d3c9d9141c7fe4ba0e32d5206ac4354eb87e805d8cc22e5b5b29a04b.jpg)

Honestly, we were braced to catch hell, but overall the captain praised us, stood with us a while, had a smoke.

But the watch turned out great in the end — we worked a lot, cranked winches and all that.

![](https://i.vas3k.club/d912566bba163a5c989c6da9a23dcc6bd187583179becc57a1707b0876865ac6.jpg)

By lunch we were somewhere around here, and there was still so much left! At best another 1.5 days.

We all already understand that those who were flying out of Lisbon on the 21st had blown their tickets home. But I'm not worrying about that yet — I'll get there somehow; first I need to reach Portugal.

And meanwhile we're bouncing on the waves here, the rolling is such that I don't want to do anything — not in the galley, not anywhere. I just sit in the saloon during the day and [watch](https://youtube.com/shorts/rYJJnkmIpxs).

At night the heel was so bad that the couch Max sits on tore loose. During the day our fire extinguisher went off, because the safety pin fell out and it spat a bit of foam while we were having lunch. The captain got pretty triggered by this — handed out a few choice words for the mess we'd made here and for treating the yacht badly — though I didn't quite agree with him on that, but I didn't argue, since it all just gave me hysterical laughter; my exhaustion was showing, honestly.

By evening some guests flew in to us — swallows, very tired, they were practically falling onto the deck. We took a piece of line and rigged them a rest area. So they'd stay with us until the morning of the next day.

![](https://i.vas3k.club/04a3033fd5494177fc82a3d6e3eb4543fcf0e77e03a4d36c7f56832bcd131b02.jpg)

## Tuesday, April 21st

On my night watch the wind finally started turning us; it was almost a full broad reach. Overall it blew a bit, but not for long. I went to crash in the closet.

I woke up rested. And that means the wind had died and we were running purely on the engine. I came up to the cockpit and saw the sails were stowed. It warmed up, the sun came out, and we decided to hang up some things to dry.

This whole time, while sailing from Tenerife, we'd been trying to catch a fish. The lines were cast practically the entire time. But this morning, realizing we wouldn't catch a damn thing, Kirill pulled out a six-pack of San Miguel (which he'd stashed in case we caught a tuna) and treated us to a beer. It was divine.

![](https://i.vas3k.club/f89715082e69ce9a2aae954e9a4c0b80bbc809c7f8639480cf467c7db12d9388.jpg)

We were approaching a traffic separation zone with active shipping. That zone is almost 20 miles.

![](https://i.vas3k.club/5e1f99a74456b8a13c175d8e9c3f3f98c053b170415bbc32aa0af974c4bcf09e.jpg)

I decide to book a night at a hotel in Lagos and buy a ticket to Warsaw for April 23rd.

But damn! A couple of hours later we find out Lagos won't take us, because there's no room in the marina, and that we're now headed to Portimão, which is +2 hours of sailing.

In the evening Zhenya and I cook dinner: Canarian potatoes and something else.

![](https://i.vas3k.club/266a71bb1d7eb1552031758fb673bc04c3d2599c88bdb51d1a4385b12e271d4a.jpg)

A couple more words about the rolling: when you're under sail, the rolling only tosses you vertically; when you're on the waves under engine alone, you get thrown in all 4 directions.

## Wednesday, April 22nd

Around midnight we started prepping the fenders, mooring lines, and all that. We entered the marina in complete darkness, basically by feel. We moored alongside at the guest dock, hooked up electricity and water.

A late dinner, and we celebrated the successful completion of the first leg of the trip.

Up at 10 in the morning; we need to vacate the cabins for the newly arrived crew members, who'd been marinating here in Lagos for three or four days because of our delay.

I unload practically all my stuff and haul it to the laundromat.

![](https://i.vas3k.club/f27bfa2b0e97eb877fc221b10d64aaa8bf158f59a70bf7a6156dbbe8e9db8800.jpg)

Around 15:00 I say my goodbyes and take a taxi to Lagos. I check into the hotel, pack my things into a banana box, and send it via DPD to Riga. 46 euros and no hassle with carry-on, especially since I didn't need any of it in Warsaw.

## The End

After Lagos I was seasick for another five days or so — you stand on solid ground, and you're still swaying.

I only re-listened to my voice memos a month later — that tired, often angry voice — I didn't recognize myself. I'll need to compile it and post an audio version.

In the end, I'm glad I didn't get off at Madeira and that I finished this nearly 1,100-mile adventure.

The crew was awesome, not a single conflict, cohesion maxed out.
